Highlight: Whether you have a sore throat or sprain or need major surgery, you want a doctor you can trust. Our list can help you find a good one. Here are the area's top 1,900 physicians in 39 specialities, as chosen by their peers.
Body


When you need to see a doctor, you might seek a referral from     your trusted primary-care physician for the best doctor or specialist in a     certain field. This spring, we asked nearly 13,000 doctors in DC,     Maryland, and Virginia for just that-about one another. Through an online     survey, the region's physicians nominated their colleagues in 39     specialties. On the following pages, you'll find a vetted list of top     vote-getters.
To vote, each physician had to include a current     medical-license number registered in either the District, Maryland, or     Virginia. Doctors could submit just one ballot each and could not vote for     themselves.
Many physicians have more than one office; we've listed the     address and phone number for only the main location. Also included are     special interests as provided by the doctor's office. Absent from the     roster are emergency-room doctors, radiologists, and anesthesiologists     because normally they're chosen by another doctor-or left to chance, as     when you're rushed to the ER or sent for an x-ray.
We hope you find the list to be a useful resource when looking     for medical care and that it helps keep you in good health.
Separate votes are taken for the top pediatric specialists in     some categories. Doctors with a "pediatrics" designation were voted onto     the list specifically as pediatric specialists.

On a diagnosis that didn't turn out to be what he thought it     would be:
"In 2010, I met a patient in her sixties who had a several-year     history of seizures. She had been well controlled on seizure medicine but     came to me because she'd been having more seizure-like episodes and some     memory difficulty. After the usual tests, we diagnosed Alzheimer's     disease. This progressed slowly until 2014, when she suddenly started to     have a lot more seizure-like episodes and cognitive changes. At that time,     I believed it was just the progression of her previously diagnosed     dementia. But she was found to have contracted Whipple disease, a rare     infectious disease that causes primarily gastrointestinal symptoms but can     cause seizures and cognitive changes. She was treated with antibiotics,     and her symptoms improved significantly. A common medical saying goes, 'If     you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras.' This was a good reminder     that sometimes it actually is a zebra."
Eric Jeffries

On the latest in cancer research:
"We think cancer in general, and colon cancer specifically,     might have a great deal to do with what's called our microbiomes-bacteria     that live in and around our colons. Over millions of years, humans have     evolved to keep in our mouths, in our skin, and in our GI tracts this     coral-reef colony of bacteria. Different people have different coral     reefs, different colonies. We know some bacteria are protective and some     are not. In fact, antibiotic use in ear infections among children, all the     hand-washing we're doing-quite honestly, we're not eating enough dirt,     we're not feeding our microbiome to balance it out. By studying this, we     understand more about how it's affecting our immune system. Why do some     people get colon cancer and some don't? We're hopeful that in     understanding more about these bacteria, we'll learn more about medicine     and cancer. If immune oncology was sort of last year, microbiome is this     year."

On technological innovations in his field:
"In neurosurgery, we have the absolute coolest tools. The stuff     once thought of as science fiction is becoming a reality with the use of     brain/computer interfaces and exoskeletons, advanced intraoperative     imaging techniques, and robots. We are removing brain tumors and treating     complex spinal conditions with minimally invasive techniques. In addition,     we are able to track the whole surgical journey of the patient with     smartphone apps and remote-monitoring technology that elevate the quality     of perioperative care. The practice of medicine is evolving constantly,     particularly neurosurgery, where the rapid technological innovation and     explosive growth of data make keeping up difficult. If your practice has     not changed ten years from now, you're falling behind."

I Don't See My Doctor's Name Here!
Should I switch?
If your physician isn't on our list, that doesn't mean he or     she isn't providing good care. Some doctors who practice in large hospital     departments aren't part of normal referral networks and may often go     unnamed. By the same token, some may work at smaller practices or are     younger and not as widely known. If you like your doctor, it makes sense     to continue that relationship.
Isn't this just a big popularity contest?
In some ways, yes. Asking for a referral means your doctor will     probably give you the name of the first physician who comes to mind, and     this person is likely to be in your doctor's circle. One difference here:     It's a good bet 13,000 doctors means a broader circle of professional     associates.
Can doctors buy their way onto the list?
No. All physicians are peer-nominated and earned their place     here. They may have also bought an ad, but this in no way guarantees a     spot on the list.
I once went to one of the doctors on your list and it was a     terrible experience.
While physicians may vote for the colleagues they respect,     their experience interacting with them might be limited, so we recommend     that you use good judgment. If you feel uncomfortable with a doctor,     despite his or her credentials, find another.
