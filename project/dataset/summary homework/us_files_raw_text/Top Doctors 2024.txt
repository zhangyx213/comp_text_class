Body


If your husband needed back surgery or your child suddenly had allergies or you noticed a suspicious mole, you'd want a good physician you could trust.
To find that person, you might ask your primary-care doctor for a recommendation. After all, physicians see one another's work and hear feedback from patients. To determine the area's Top Doctors, we also relied on expert referrals-we sent ballots to more than 13,000 physicians and asked which of their peers they'd choose for their own family's healthcare. The result is this list of more than 2,100 physicians in 43 specialties.
We set a few ground rules: Doctors could submit only one ballot, couldn't vote for themselves or their partners, and had to provide a current medical-license number to vote. (For more on the process, see page 90.)For each listing, we give the address and phone number for the physician's main office (many have multiple locations) and, for those who provided them, some special interests.
Just because a doctor's name appears here doesn't mean that person will be a good fit for you. Consider this one resource to help make any medical odyssey a little easier to navigate.

Wait! My Doctor Isn't on the List!
I don't see my physician's name here. Should I go to someone else?
There's no need to switch if you like and trust your doctor. There are reasons why a perfectly good practitioner may not have made this list. Doctors who are newer, in smaller practices, or in out-of-the-way locations may not have as large a referral network and so might not receive as many nominations.
Can doctors get on the list by buying an ad?
Absolutely not. The only way someone can get on is by receiving enough nominations from peers. Once the votes were tallied, physicians were notified that they'd been named a Top Doctor, and some chose to buy advertising. But many did not.
I don't see some specialities, like anesthesiology.
This list is intended as a guide to help you select a physician. That's why you won't find anesthesiologists, diagnostic radiologists, ER doctors, and other specialists usually chosen for you, often in an emergency. We also tend not to list fields with relatively few practitioners to vote for. And we don't include complementary and alternative medicine, such as acupuncture and chiropractic.
I was treated by a doctor on this list and it was an awful experience.
We're sorry. Physicians are voted onto the list by peers who can speak to medical skill but who can't always know if, say, a surgeon has a dismissive bedside manner, an ob-gyn has a rude receptionist, or a dermatologist's billing department is disorganized. Ultimately, you're the best judge of whether a medical practice is right for you.
Don't doctors just vote for their friends?
They may vote for colleagues who happen to be friends, but that doesn't mean those people aren't qualified to be on the list. If you ask a doctor for a referral, they'll likely suggest someone they know and trust. That said, we researched every name-checking, for example, that the physician didn't have any recent malpractice judgments or disciplinary actions from a local medical board. We also looked at, and adjusted for, suspicious voting patterns.
