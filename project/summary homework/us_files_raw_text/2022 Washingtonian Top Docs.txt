Highlight: More than 2,000 of the region's best physicians, chosen by their peers. Did your doctor make the list?
Body


If your child has broken an arm or your back pain won't quit or you need a second opinion after a scary diagnosis, you want a physician you can trust. You want to be in the best hands possible. · What follows is our annual list of the area's Top Doctors. The more than 2,000 practitioners in 42 specialties were selected by their peers. · To come up with the list, we sent online ballots to more than 13,000 physicians in the greater Washington area and asked them to nominate fellow physicians they would choose for themselves or their loved ones. 
Doctors could submit only one ballot, could not vote for themselves or their partners, and had to provide a current medical-license number to vote. · For each listing, we give the address and phone number of the physician's main office (many have multiple locations) and, for those who provided them, some special interests. You won't find radiologists, ER doctors, or anesthesiologists here because those aren't specialists you typically need to choose-they're usually selected by the physician performing the procedure or at random in emergency situations. · Just because a doctor is listed here doesn't mean that person is right for you. Yet we hope the list is a helpful resource that can ease some of the anxiety of finding good medical care.
B. Elizabeth Delasobera sports medicine
What's the one healthy habit you recommend starting?
"Of course, the sports-medicine doctor would say exercise is the best medicine, but beyond that, my advice is to change up what you do. I see a lot of overuse injuries due to always doing the same type of exercise. If you do some weight-bearing and some non-weight-bearing exercise, some strength work, some flexibility work, some core work, some cardio/endurance work, you will prevent being sidelined due to pain and overuse."
David W. Patterson internal medicine
It's been a hard two years in healthcare. What keeps you going?
"Even though the pandemic has stressed our healthcare system, every day continues to remind me of why I went into medicine in the first place. Being able to help in a time of major upheaval makes coming in every day very enjoyable."
Noreen Galaria dermatology
What excites you about the future of your field?
"The future of dermatology and aesthetics is regenerative. With new and emerging therapies, we will soon be able to slow the sands of time. Ralph V. Boccia oncology and hematology

What's the one healthy habit you recommend starting?
"Self-care. This means being selfish and forcing yourself to take some time to eat well-focus on growing a great gut micro­biome-exercising, and sleeping. I learned the hard way how important this is. I had a massive heart attack and needed a triple bypass at age 45. Thankfully, I've been fortunate enough to be given a second chance and to realize how important it is to live a balanced lifestyle."

What do you love most about your specialty?
"The aspect of urology that is most attractive is that the patient's problem is almost always clearly defined. For example, a patient who presents with the inability to urinate: We can pinpoint the etiology and then offer a solution. Unlike other specialties where the diagnosis is more nebulous, most of our patients get better after treatment, which is very rewarding."
Jay Won Rhee neurosurgery
What do you love most about your specialty?
"I love the complexity and consistency of the nervous system. How neurologic functions travel to and from specific regions of the brain and body through the spinal cord and nerves, near instantaneously. We are often able to diagnose the precise location within the nervous system responsible for problems with a targeted neurologic exam and history from the patient before the use of imaging studies or other diagnostic technologies."
Wait! My Doctor's Not on the List!
Should I get a new doctor?
There's no need to switch if you like and trust your physician. There are reasons why some doctors might not get enough votes from peers. If they're in an out-of-the-way location, in a small or new practice, or have recently moved to the area, fewer doctors will know their work well enough to nominate them.
I was treated by one of the doctors on this list, and it was an awful experience.
We're sorry. While physicians who see their work have judged the doctors on this list to have excellent medical skills, those filling out our ballots don't always know if, say, a peer's bedside manner leaves something to be desired or if the receptionist is rude. In the end, you are the best judge of whether a doctor is right for you.
Can doctors get on this list by buying an ad?
Absolutely not. They get on the list only one way-by receiving enough votes from their peers. Once this year's roster was done, physicians were notified that they had been named a Top Doctor, and some chose to buy advertising. But many did not.
Don't physicians just vote for their friends?
It is possible that doctors vote for practitioners they also consider friends, but that doesn't mean those people aren't qualified to be on the list. If you ask physicians for a referral, they're likely to suggest someone they know and like. But rest assured that each doctor needs a certain number of votes to make the list and that we also look at, and adjust for, suspicious voting patterns.
