Highlight: Whom should you see for your aching back, your child's allergies, or a suspicious mole? Whether you need a new doctor for a routine exam or a second opinion on a diagnosis, you want a physician you can trust. Here are 1,900 doctors in 39 specialties-the region's very best, as chosen by their peers.
Body


When you need a cardiologist or breast-cancer surgeon or     neurologist, you would probably ask your primary-care physician whom he or     she would recommend. For this list of the area's Top Doctors, we did just     that, on a bigger scale: We sent surveys to nearly 12,000 physicians in     DC, Maryland, and Virginia and asked whom they would trust to provide care     for their patients.
On the following pages, you'll find the local doctors, in 39     specialties, who received the most recommendations from their     peers.
We conducted this survey online, and each electronic ballot had     to include a current, local medical-license number to be counted. Each     physician could submit just one ballot.
The physicians listed practice throughout the Washington     area-from inside the Beltway to well outside. While some have multiple     offices, we've included the full address and phone number for only their     main office. We've also given special interests as provided by the     physician's office.
You won't find anesthesiologists, diagnostic radiologists, or     emergency-room physicians here, because they're normally chosen by another     doctor or by circumstance. The list is intended as a resource to help     patients find good medical care. We hope it helps keep you in good     health.

" My doctor's not on the list?! "
I don't see my doctor here. Should I find a different     one?
Don't worry if your physician is missing from our list-it     doesn't mean he or she isn't worthy or the best provider for you. If you     like and trust the doctors you see now, it makes sense to maintain those     relationships, whether or not they're listed. Many first-rate doctors     don't appear here for a variety of reasons.
What reasons? Why would a good doctor be left off the     list?
Doctors typically are known within the medical communities     where they practice and make referrals. Physicians in one referral circle     often don't know those in others. In a peer survey like ours, this can be     a disadvantage for doctors in out-of-the-way locations and in "closed"     organizations where outside referrals are limited, or for those with very     narrow subspecialties.
Many who practice in-and even lead-hospital departments aren't     part of normal referral networks and often don't receive votes. Many     younger physicians haven't been in practice long enough to establish their     reputations. What's more, doctors in group practices often are thought of     collectively-sometimes only one or two of the "name" physicians in a group     make the list while their associates, perhaps similarly qualified, don't     earn enough votes.
I see doctor ads in your magazine. Can't a physician just buy     his way onto the list?
Never. There's a very clear line in the sand at     Washingtonian between advertising and editorial.
I once saw one of the doctors on your list and thought he was     awful.
Although the physicians listed here are respected by their     colleagues, inclusion is no guarantee you'll be satisfied, nor should the     list be a substitute for your own judgment. Almost every physician has     difficult outcomes over the course of a career, as well as some     dissatisfied patients. While professional peers may be able to assess     knowledge and skill, they aren't necessarily able to judge bedside manner.     If you feel uncomfortable with a doctor, no matter his or her credentials,     find another.
